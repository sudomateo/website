"You need to have taste when using AI." I've heard this sentiment countless times over the past year, usually delivered with the confidence of someone who's discovered a profound truth. The implication is clear: AI is a powerful tool, but without proper taste, you'll produce garbage. Fair enough. But here's what bothers me about this advice—most people complaining about the need for taste with AI never applied taste before AI anyway.

## The Taste Paradox

Let's be honest about something. The same people warning about AI's potential for tasteless output are often the same people who:

- Copy-pasted Stack Overflow answers without understanding them
- Used generic WordPress themes with no customization
- Wrote emails that could have been generated by a template
- Created PowerPoint presentations that looked like they came from 2003
- Built websites that screamed "I used the first Bootstrap example I found"

Where was the taste then? Where was the careful curation, the thoughtful consideration, the refined judgment that supposedly separates good work from mediocre work?

The truth is, most people have been producing tasteless work for years. AI didn't create this problem—it just made it more visible and scalable. Now that everyone can generate content at the speed of thought, we're suddenly concerned about quality control. It's like complaining about fast food while having eaten nothing but microwave dinners for the past decade.

## What Is Taste, Really?

This brings us to a more fundamental question: what exactly does taste mean when it comes to technology and AI? The word gets thrown around so casually that it's lost most of its meaning. Is taste about aesthetics? Quality? Appropriateness? Cultural sensitivity? Technical excellence?

In the context of AI, I think taste manifests in several ways:

**Contextual Appropriateness**: Knowing when AI-generated content fits the situation and when it doesn't. Using AI to draft a casual email to a colleague? Probably fine. Using AI to write a heartfelt condolence message? Maybe not.

**Quality Recognition**: Being able to distinguish between AI output that's genuinely useful and output that's just plausible-sounding nonsense. This requires domain knowledge—you can't have taste about something you don't understand.

**Iterative Refinement**: Understanding that AI is a starting point, not an endpoint. Good taste means knowing how to prompt, edit, and refine AI output until it meets your standards.

**Ethical Boundaries**: Recognizing when AI use crosses lines of authenticity, fairness, or respect. This is perhaps the most important aspect of AI taste.

But here's the thing—these aren't new skills. They're the same skills we should have been applying to all our work all along. The only difference is that AI has made the consequences of poor judgment more immediate and visible.

## Depth vs. Breadth: The Taste Spectrum

This leads to an interesting question about the nature of taste itself. Should we focus on developing deep taste in specific domains, or broad taste across many areas?

**Depth of taste** means becoming exceptionally discerning within a particular field. A software engineer with deep taste might be able to spot subtle code quality issues that others miss, or recognize when an AI-generated algorithm is elegant versus merely functional. This kind of taste comes from years of experience and deep domain knowledge.

**Breadth of taste** means developing general principles of quality and appropriateness that apply across domains. Someone with broad taste might not be able to evaluate the technical merits of a machine learning model, but they can recognize when AI-generated content feels authentic, useful, and appropriate for its intended audience.

Both approaches have merit, but I'd argue that breadth of taste is more valuable in the AI era. Here's why: AI democratizes creation across domains. A marketing professional might now find themselves generating code, a developer might create marketing copy, and a designer might write technical documentation. In this world, having broad principles of quality and appropriateness becomes more valuable than deep expertise in any single area.

The most effective AI users I know aren't necessarily the most technically sophisticated. They're the people who have developed a general sense of what good work looks like, regardless of the medium. They can recognize when something feels off, even if they can't articulate exactly why. They understand their own limitations and know when to seek expert review.

## The Real Challenge

The real challenge isn't developing taste for AI—it's developing taste, period. AI is just the latest tool that amplifies our existing judgment, for better or worse. If you had poor taste before AI, you'll have poor taste with AI. If you had good taste before AI, you'll likely adapt that taste to work effectively with AI.

Instead of focusing on "AI taste" as some new skill we need to develop, maybe we should focus on the fundamentals: understanding quality, developing judgment, learning to iterate and refine, and building the domain knowledge necessary to evaluate output in our areas of work.

The people who are most successful with AI aren't the ones who've developed some mystical new sense of "AI taste." They're the people who already had good judgment and have simply applied that judgment to a new tool. The taste was always there—AI just gave them a new canvas to paint on.

So the next time someone tells you that you need taste to use AI effectively, ask them: where was that taste before AI came along? And more importantly, what are you doing to develop it now?
